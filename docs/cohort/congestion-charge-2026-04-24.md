## Summary

This cohort of 11 residents and small business owners, interviewed about a proposed congestion charge / weekend car closure, reveals a consistent pattern: people are already paying costs — rerouting journeys, moving appointments, losing customers, absorbing guilt — before any policy is live. The most load-bearing finding is that dependents are the pressure point: mothers-in-law needing hospital transport, grandchildren with asthma on school runs, elderly neighbours relying on car-based informal care, and Saturday regulars from outer areas sustaining independent shops. Support for the principle (air quality, vibrancy) is broad but fragile once lived costs are articulated. Trust in the consultation process is low across the cohort — residents describe discovering the scheme by chance, filling forms 'into the void', and self-censoring for fear of being miscategorised as anti-progress. Several participants explicitly shifted mid-interview from 'the council isn't listening' to 'I haven't actually engaged' — suggesting the passivity itself is a process artefact worth naming.

## Top themes
- preemptive adaptation before policy is live
- dependents as the unvoiced pressure point (mothers-in-law, grandchildren, elderly neighbours)
- health-access barriers (hospital appointments, asthma exposure on school runs)
- consultation format mismatch — decisions land, not dialogue
- Camden 30% figure as cohort-wide borrowed anxiety
- class/fairness framing: 'ordinary working people hit first'
- existential vs. economic arguments — which is 'allowed' to count
- self-censorship to avoid being seen as anti-progress
- small-business vulnerability via dispersed Saturday customer base
- the irony of rerouting: creating the congestion the scheme claims to solve

## Routing recommendations (6)

### Scheme design / exemptions team
The cohort's load-bearing concerns cluster around dependents with essential medical trips (mother-in-law hospital appointments, child asthma) and informal carer access. Most participants do not know whether medical, carer, or mobility exemptions exist — and are making irreversible behaviour changes (switching hospitals, providers) in that information vacuum. A clearly-communicated exemption and verification pathway would directly defuse several of the cohort's strongest objections.

_Supporting: 5 sessions_

### Public health / children's respiratory health leads
Two sessions independently describe children with GP-diagnosed asthma whose symptoms correlate daily with peak-hour traffic exposure on specific named stretches (Riverside Drive / Riverside). Carers are already absorbing time and fuel costs to mitigate exposure. This is a concrete public-health case that sits outside standard 'congestion charge' framing.

_Supporting: 3 sessions_

### Public transport / bus service planning
Trust repair for at least one strongly affected working-carer participant is explicitly conditional on early-morning / late-evening bus routes compatible with shift work and school runs — buses that apparently do not currently exist at required times. The charge is seen as a tax without a viable alternative unless this is addressed first.

_Supporting: 2 sessions_

### Small business / high street economy lead
Independent retailers describe a structural vulnerability the scheme may not have modelled: their Saturday viability depends on dispersed outer-area repeat customers (not local foot traffic), who can easily substitute to online or out-of-town retail. Secondary effect: Saturday revenue subsidises weekday stocking for local customers, so the harm cascades. Camden's 30% figure is circulating cohort-wide as anxiety, suggesting a communication need.

_Supporting: 3 sessions_

### Consultation / engagement design team
The cohort's most consistent trust finding is not that the council won't listen, but that the consultation format doesn't reach them and the binary form doesn't fit their lived reality. Multiple participants describe self-censoring for fear of being miscategorised as anti-progress. A redesigned engagement mechanism — outreach to shopkeepers in situ, conversations rather than yes/no forms, and visible feedback loops — would likely shift whether the supportive-but-concerned majority engages at all.

_Supporting: 6 sessions_

### Equalities / adult social care lead
Informal caring relationships (adult children driving mothers-in-law to hospital; neighbours doing shopping for housebound elderly) function as invisible accessibility infrastructure that the scheme appears not to have mapped. These carers don't self-identify as carers and don't know which representative channels apply to them. Worth a dedicated impact assessment.

_Supporting: 4 sessions_
