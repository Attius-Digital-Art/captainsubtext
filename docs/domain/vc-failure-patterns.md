# VC Failure Taxonomy for Founder Interviews

Reference doc embedded in the Founder Investment Evaluation brief. Drawn from CB Insights post-mortem analysis (2023, n=110 startup failures), First Round Capital founder diligence patterns (2022), and Sequoia's internal "company building" failure classification.

The point of this document is not prediction — it's calibration. Interviewers who know these patterns can probe the *precursors* to each failure mode before they crystallize.

---

## The five categories that account for 90%+ of failures

### 1. No market need (42% of failures)

**The pattern:** The founder built something technically correct that nobody needed urgently enough to pay for. Often: the pain was real but episodic, or the workaround was "good enough," or the buyer didn't control budget.

**What founders say:** "The product worked great, we just had trouble finding the right customer." Translation: the problem wasn't acute enough to force buying behavior.

**Probes:**
- "How often does this specific problem cost your target customer something tangible — time, money, a relationship?"
- "What are they doing today instead of buying your product? How good is that workaround?"
- "Have you seen a customer *not* adopt your product and explain why? What was the reason?"

**Signal to watch for:** Founder who pivots to feature comparison when asked about urgency. Urgency is about the *problem*, not the solution.

---

### 2. Team issues (23% of failures)

**The pattern:** The founding team had the wrong composition for the problem — not a capability gap (expertise can be hired), but a *judgment and temperament* gap that compounds over time. Includes: founder-market fit, cofounder dysfunction, and "know-it-all" founders who can't learn.

**What founders say:** "My cofounder and I decided to go separate ways." Rarely: "I was wrong about the market and couldn't update."

**Probes:**
- "Tell me about the last time you significantly changed your view on how the product should work. What caused the change?"
- "Where in this problem are you most likely to be wrong? What would you need to see to know you were wrong?"
- "What do your best customers not like about the product?"

**Signal to watch for:** Founder who hedges all negative signals ("not many customers said that" / "that was an outlier"). Inability to cite a specific moment of learning from failure is a yellow flag.

---

### 3. Ran out of cash (29% of failures, often proximate not ultimate)

**The pattern:** Running out of cash is usually a symptom, not a cause. The underlying cause is: customers didn't pay, or didn't pay fast enough, or the cost of acquiring them exceeded what they paid.

**The founder-market fit version:** Founders who are technically strong but inexperienced with sales underestimate the length and cost of the enterprise sales cycle.

**Probes:**
- "Walk me through the last three deals you closed. Who said yes, who said no, how long did each take?"
- "What's your customer acquisition cost today, and how has it changed over the past 6 months?"
- "What's the typical time from first demo to signed contract for an enterprise customer?"

**Signal to watch for:** Founder who conflates "we have strong interest" with "we have customers." Interest is free. Contracts are evidence.

---

### 4. Competitive pressure (19% of failures)

**The pattern:** The market worked. The technology worked. But a better-resourced competitor entered with the same insight and outspent on distribution.

**What founders say:** "We got beaten by [large company]." More concerning: "We didn't see them coming."

**Probes:**
- "Who are the next three companies to build this? Why haven't they already?"
- "If [large company] decided to build your product, what would you do?"
- "What's your defensibility in 24 months — what will be harder for a newcomer to replicate?"

**Signal to watch for:** Founder who competes on features, not on switching cost, distribution, or network effects. Features are copyable; the others aren't (as fast).

---

### 5. Wrong pricing / business model (18% of failures, frequently underweighted)

**The pattern:** The product created value but couldn't capture it. Wrong unit of pricing, wrong buyer, wrong contract structure. Often shows up as: high usage, low revenue, or high revenue, high churn.

**Probes:**
- "How did you decide what to charge? When did you first charge something, and what happened?"
- "Who in your customer organization approves the budget for this purchase?"
- "Have you tried a different pricing model? What happened?"

## What "founder-market fit" actually means

Not domain expertise — that can be hired. The question is: does this founder have the *specific type of judgment* the market will punish you for lacking?

CB Insights' post-mortems show that founders who fail at "no market need" had usually:
1. Talked to customers who *wanted* the product but didn't *need* it urgently
2. Confused early adopter enthusiasm with product-market fit
3. Never tested the thing that would kill the product: the workaround

The interview is designed to find the gap between what the founder believes and what the evidence supports — not to catch founders out, but to understand whether they've done the work that precedes genuine conviction.
