# Civic Participation Frameworks for Consultation Interviews

Reference doc embedded in the Civic Consultation brief. Draws on Arnstein (1969), IAP2 Spectrum (2018), and the academic literature on participation failure modes. Translated into operational interviewer guidance.

---

## Arnstein's Ladder of Participation (1969)

The ladder describes eight rungs, divided into three bands. The conductor uses this to understand *where in the ladder* a participant believes their engagement sits, and whether that matches the host's intent.

### Non-participation (rungs 1–2)

| Rung | Name | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Manipulation | Officials use "participation" to educate or persuade. No genuine two-way exchange. |
| 2 | Therapy | Officials treat citizen concerns as deficits to be corrected, not inputs to be processed. |

**Interview signal:** Participant describes previous consultations where they were "told, not asked." This is rung 1–2 experience. Their current engagement is shaped by this history.

---

### Tokenism (rungs 3–5)

| Rung | Name | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| 3 | Informing | Citizens receive information. No obligation to act on responses. |
| 4 | Consultation | Citizens are asked to give opinions. Officials may or may not act on them. |
| 5 | Placation | A few token representatives are included but remain outnumbered and can be overridden. |

**Interview signal:** "They told us they'd take our feedback into account" is rung 3–4 language. The participant may not know whether anything will change. Probe: "What would make you confident this process is different from a consultation where the decision was already made?"

---

### Citizen power (rungs 6–8)

| Rung | Name | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| 6 | Partnership | Power is redistributed via negotiation. Citizens have formal role in decision-making. |
| 7 | Delegated power | Citizens hold majority decision-making authority for a defined scope. |
| 8 | Citizen control | Citizens govern themselves for the defined scope. |

**Interview signal:** Participants who have experienced rungs 6–8 describe specific mechanisms — veto rights, formal seats at tables, defined scope of authority. Abstract claims ("we have real influence") without mechanism description are usually rung 4–5.

## IAP2 Spectrum of Public Participation (2018)

The IAP2 Spectrum is more operational than Arnstein's Ladder — it distinguishes participation modes by what the organizer commits to doing with input.

| Level | Commitment | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Inform | "We'll keep you informed" | Environmental impact report |
| Consult | "We'll listen and provide feedback on how your input influenced the decision" | Planning consultation |
| Involve | "We'll work with you throughout the process" | Community design charrette |
| Collaborate | "We'll incorporate your advice and recommendations to the maximum extent possible" | Participatory budgeting |
| Empower | "We'll implement what you decide" | Community land trust |

**Critical distinction from Arnstein:** IAP2 focuses on the *organizer's commitment*, not the *outcome*. A "consult" that actually changed a decision is still a "consult" in IAP2 terms.

## Known participation failure modes

These are failure patterns that appear across multiple studies of civic consultation. The conductor uses them to probe whether the participant has experienced any of them.

### 1. Participation fatigue

**What it looks like:** Community members who have been through multiple consultations with no visible outcome stop engaging. "They've asked us before and nothing changed."

**Interview signal:** Participant references previous consultations before describing this one. Their current level of engagement reflects this history, not just the present process.

**Probe:** "How does this process compare to the last time something like this was proposed in your area? What's similar, what's different?"

---

### 2. The usual suspects problem

**What it looks like:** The same 10–20 community members show up to every consultation. They're engaged and vocal. They represent themselves, not the community.

**Structural cause:** Consultations favor participants who are available during business hours, comfortable in formal settings, and already networked with organizers.

**Interview signal:** "I've been going to these meetings for ten years." This participant is engaged but may represent a narrow slice.

**Probe:** "Who in your neighborhood *doesn't* come to these meetings? Why not? What would it take for them to be here?"

---

### 3. Predetermined outcome framing

**What it looks like:** The "choices" offered to participants are all variants of the preferred option. The consultation is structured to produce a predetermined result while appearing participatory.

**Interview signal:** Participant describes the options as "pick which version of [X]" without any option that would reverse or substantially change [X].

**Probe:** "Was there an option on the table that would have meant the project doesn't go ahead? What happened to that option?"

---

### 4. Technical language capture

**What it looks like:** Consultation documents are written in planning/legal/engineering language. Community members can't evaluate the tradeoffs without expertise they don't have. Comment periods favor those who can respond in technical language.

**Interview signal:** Participant describes difficulty understanding the materials or having to rely on someone else to explain them.

**Probe:** "When you first looked at the plans, what did you understand and what didn't you understand? How did you figure out what you needed to know?"

---

### 5. Post-decision consultation

**What it looks like:** Community is consulted after a decision has effectively been made. The "consultation" is compliance theater.

**Timing signal:** If the consultation happens after funding is committed, contracts are signed, or planning permission is conditionally approved, the decision space is already constrained.

**Interview signal:** "They told us it was already decided." Or: "The company had already broken ground."

---

## What good consultation evidence looks like

These signals suggest genuine participation, not theater:

1. **Decision feedback loop:** Participants can point to specific ways their input changed the proposal — not "they took our concerns seriously" but "they removed the access road that would have blocked the school entrance."
2. **Reversibility signal:** Participants understand what would cause the project to be modified or cancelled, and believe that outcome is possible.
3. **Diverse voice index:** The consultation included people who don't normally show up — this requires active outreach, not open-door policy.
4. **Mechanism specificity:** When asked "how does your input get into the final decision?", participants can describe the mechanism, not just express hope.
