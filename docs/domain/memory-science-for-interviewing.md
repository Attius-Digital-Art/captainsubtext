# Memory Science for Post-Incident Interviews

Reference doc embedded in the Post-Incident Witness brief. Condensed from Loftus (2005), McNally (2003), and Schacter (2001). Each axiom is stated as an operational rule for the interviewer, not as academic theory.

---

## 1. Memory is reconstructive, not reproductive

**The claim:** Witnesses do not play back a recording. They reconstruct an event from fragments — schema, context, emotion, and post-event information all shape the reconstruction.

**Interview implication:** A witness who gives a confident, coherent account is not necessarily more accurate. Confidence and accuracy decorrelate under stress. Probe the *construction* process: "What are you basing that on — what you saw, or what you learned afterwards?"

**Conductor deployment:** When a witness gives an unusually coherent timeline of a chaotic event, flag for possible post-event contamination.

---

## 2. Source monitoring errors are common

**The claim:** People frequently misattribute the origin of a memory — whether they saw something or were told about it, whether it happened in this incident or a previous one.

**Interview implication:** Distinguish "I saw" from "I heard" from "I later found out." Probe explicitly: "Is that something you witnessed directly, or is it something you pieced together?"

**Conductor deployment:** When a witness shifts from first-person witness language ("I saw") to second-hand framing ("apparently" / "we were told"), probe the source.

---

## 3. The misinformation effect corrupts encoding

**The claim:** Post-event information — news reports, colleague conversations, management briefings — retroactively alters what the witness believes they experienced.

**Timing:** The corruption effect is strongest in the first 24–48 hours. After one week, contaminated memories are indistinguishable from original memories by the witness.

**Interview implication:** Ask what the witness knew *at the time*, not what they now understand. "Walk me through what you understood to be happening at 14:22 — not the debrief, that moment."

**Conductor deployment:** When the interview occurs more than 48 hours after the incident, explicitly bracket post-event information early.

---

## 4. Stress enhances central-event memory, impairs peripheral detail

**The claim:** Under acute stress, witnesses encode the "central" elements of the scene (the thing that caused the stress) with higher fidelity. Peripheral details (bystanders, background, time) are encoded with lower fidelity.

**Interview implication:** Do not discount a witness who is vague about peripheral details but vivid about the core event. Do probe peripheral detail gaps — they may reveal what the witness *couldn't* process in the moment.

---

## 5. The reconsolidation window (6-hour retrieval effect)

**The claim:** Each time a memory is retrieved, it briefly becomes labile — open to modification. The window is approximately 6 hours after retrieval.

**Implication for interview timing:** If a witness has already been interviewed by others, their memory of the incident now includes their *memory of recalling the incident*. Ask: "You've probably been over this a few times already. What's changed in your account from the first time you told it?"

---

## 6. Social contagion in group recall

**The claim:** When people recall events in groups, they adopt each other's memories. A confident witness's account overwrites a less confident witness's independent memory.

**Interview implication:** Never interview multiple witnesses together if accuracy is the goal. In a post-incident group debrief, the loudest or most senior person's account becomes the "canonical" account regardless of accuracy.

**Conductor deployment:** Ask whether the witness has discussed the incident with colleagues before this interview. If yes, probe which parts of their current account *predate* those conversations.

---

## The interview discipline these axioms imply

1. **Open questions first, closed questions to pin down.** Do not suggest categories before the witness generates them independently.
2. **No timeline before the witness offers one.** Imposed structure shapes what gets remembered.
3. **Name the retrieval cues you're using.** "I'm going to ask you to go back to the moment before the alarm." This reduces confabulation from subsequent events.
4. **Probe surprise.** "Was there anything that struck you as unexpected in the minutes before?" Unexpected details are more likely to be encoded independently of schema.
